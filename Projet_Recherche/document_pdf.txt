enables them to effectively cut through various materials, including the tough metals
commonly found in nuclear reactors, streamlining the dismantling process and po-
tentially lowering associated costs.[6][4].

Reactor Internals (RVIs).[8][9] Moreover, the establishment of the Laser Technocen-

Laser Technology in Nuclear Decommissioning
Laser technology has emerged as a significant advancement in the nuclear decom-
missioning industry, offering efficient and reliable solutions for dismantling nuclear
facilities. This technology is particularly relevant in managing the complexities and
hazards associated with decommissioning operations.

Advantages of Laser Technology
Laser cutting technology provides numerous benefits over traditional methods in
nuclear decommissioning. Its key advantages include precision and reliability, al-
lowing for accurate cuts without mechanical failure, which is essential in handling
radioactive materials safely[1][2]. Additionally, lasers significantly reduce the risk
of operator exposure in hazardous environments, thus enhancing safety protocols
during decommissioning activities[2].
Moreover, laser systems are versatile, capable of cutting through a variety of mate-
rials, including tough metals commonly found in nuclear facilities[2]. This versatility
makes laser technology suitable for multiple cutting applications, which can help
reduce both time and costs associated with decommissioning processes[1][8].

Case Studies and Applications
Various case studies have demonstrated the successful application of laser tech-
nology in decommissioning projects. Notably, the LaserSnake project at Sellafield
has illustrated how laser systems can facilitate the dismantling of complex struc-
tures within nuclear sites[15]. Similarly, Onet Technologies has established a laser
technocenter aimed at optimizing laser applications in dismantling projects. The
center focuses on training operators, demonstrating technology effectiveness, and
developing innovative solutions tailored for decommissioning needs[2].
The LD-SAFE project further emphasizes the global interest in laser technology,
validating its applications for decommissioning and supporting its integration into
international efforts, such as studies at the Fukushima-Daichi site[5].

Current Laser-Based Solutions in France
In France, laser technology has emerged as a significant solution for nuclear de-
commissioning, offering enhanced precision, safety, and efficiency in dismantling
nuclear facilities. This technology, largely developed by the French Atomic Energy
Authority (CEA) and industrialized by companies like Onet Technologies, is proving to
be effective in addressing the challenges associated with decommissioning nuclear
power reactors both domestically and internationally[3][4].

Advantages of Laser Technology
Laser cutting technology is recognized for its numerous advantages over traditional
decommissioning methods.
Precision and Reliability: Lasers provide high precision cutting without the mechan-
ical failures often associated with conventional methods. This accuracy is crucial in
the delicate environments of nuclear facilities[2].
Safety: Laser systems minimize operator exposure to radiation, making them partic-
ularly suitable for radioactive environments[2][7].
Versatility: Lasers can cut through a wide range of materials, including tough metals,
facilitating the efficient dismantling of nuclear reactor components[6][4].

Applications in Nuclear Decommissioning
Laser technology is being implemented in various aspects of nuclear decommission-
ing:
Decontamination Processes: Laser ablation techniques allow for the layer-by-layer
removal of contaminated surfaces, such as those affected by oxide layers. This
method captures ejected materials, preventing them from redepositing on cleaned
surfaces[7][19].
Laser Cleaning Systems: Devices such as those developed by Allied Scientific Pro
utilize high-powered lasers for effective nuclear decontamination. These systems
can clean surfaces without generating significant secondary waste, maintaining
environmental integrity[7].
Remote Operation and Dismantling: The use of remotely controlled laser systems
enhances operational safety during the dismantling of nuclear components. Onet
Technologies has pioneered processes that allow for laser cutting and decontamina-
tion from a safe distance, reducing risks to human operators[4].

Notable Projects and Developments
French companies are involved in prominent international projects that showcase the
capabilities of laser technology in nuclear decommissioning. For instance, studies at
the Fukushima-Daichi site in Japan are currently exploring the application of laser
cutting solutions, highlighting the global interest and effectiveness of this technology
in dismantling efforts[6][4].

Case Studies and Projects
LD-SAFE Project
One of the significant initiatives in the nuclear decommissioning industry is the
LD-SAFE project, which focuses on demonstrating the effectiveness of both in-air
and underwater laser cutting technologies for dismantling the most challenging
components of nuclear reactors, specifically the Reactor Pressure Vessel (RPV) and
Reactor Internals (RVI). The LD-SAFE project aims to show that laser cutting is
as safe, if not safer, than conventional cutting methods currently in use, while also
being more cost-effective and simpler to implement on-site[8][9]. The project intends
to eliminate the remaining technical, safety, and economic barriers that prevent the
widespread adoption of laser cutting in nuclear dismantling[8].

Robotics in Nuclear Dismantling
The integration of advanced robotics into nuclear decommissioning processes has
proven essential in enhancing safety and efficiency. Companies like Framatome uti-
lize specialized robotic systems, including robotic arms and laser robots, to undertake
strenuous tasks during decommissioning operations. For example, the Eloise robot
has been employed for dismantling operations at the Superphénix reactor, while
Framatome's Charli robot handles tasks such as sorting radioactive waste and cutting
contaminated pipework remotely[12]. These innovations allow for a reduction in
operator exposure to contamination and improve the overall productivity and reliability
of the dismantling process[12].

France’s Strategic Vision for a Sustainable Nuclear Future
France is committed to a strategic vision for sustainable nuclear energy, prominently
featuring the closed fuel cycle concept, which emphasizes the reprocessing of used
nuclear fuel to recover reusable materials and minimize waste volume[13]. In this
context, the integration of advanced technologies, including robotics and digital twins
of nuclear facilities, plays a crucial role. For instance, the CA2AN project, managed by
Andra, employs digital replicas of nuclear facilities to enhance safety and efficiency
in waste management, enabling operators to optimize workflows and conduct virtual
training[12].

Innovative Projects at Orano
At the Orano site in Bessines-sur-Gartempe, pilot projects are underway focusing on
innovative processes that aim to produce electricity from recycled ores and electric
vehicle batteries. These initiatives highlight the evolving landscape of nuclear decom-
missioning and the ongoing commitment to sustainability in the nuclear industry[13].
The development of such innovative solutions is supported by significant investments
from governmental initiatives, showcasing France's dedication to advancing nuclear
technology and addressing the challenges associated with waste management[13].
Through these case studies and projects, the French nuclear decommissioning
industry demonstrates a robust commitment to leveraging innovative technologies,
including laser-based solutions and robotics, to address the complex challenges of
dismantling nuclear facilities while promoting safety and efficiency.

Regulatory and Safety Considerations
The regulatory framework governing laser-based solutions in the nuclear decom-
missioning industry in France is shaped by stringent safety standards and a com-
prehensive application process. France's Nuclear Safety and Radiation Protection
Authority (ASNR) plays a crucial role in assessing and validating the safety cases
presented by decommissioning projects, including those utilizing innovative laser
technologies[10]. Following the merger of ASN and IRSN in January 2025, ASNR
undertook a technical review of the Cigéo project, which encompasses the long-term
management of radioactive waste. This review is structured around three main
themes: geological environment safety assessments, operational phase evaluations,
and post-closure safety considerations, with public inquiries anticipated as part of the
licensing process[10].
Furthermore, since 1990, French regulations have mandated decommissioning pro-
visions that necessitate licensees to justify their safety requirements within decom-
missioning applications[11]. The emphasis on safety is underscored by projects
like LD-SAFE, which aims to eliminate barriers to the adoption of laser cutting
technologies in reactor dismantling by ensuring compliance with safety and regu-
latory standards[8][9]. The ambition is to enhance safety, reduce radioactive waste,
and improve cost-effectiveness, addressing both technical and economic hurdles
associated with conventional methods[8][2].
In addition, the integration of advanced technologies such as “digital twins” further
supports safety and efficiency in waste management operations. These digital repli-
cas enable real-time monitoring and optimization of workflows, thereby mitigating
risks associated with radioactive materials[12]. As regulatory frameworks continue
to evolve, the recognition of laser-based methods as viable solutions for nuclear site
cleanup is gaining traction, reinforcing the importance of adhering to safety standards
established by both national and international bodies[20].
